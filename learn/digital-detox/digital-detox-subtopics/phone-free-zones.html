Phone-Free Zones

Author: Jazztine Kurt U. Ramos

Source: https://screentimelabs.com/blog/yondr-phone-free-zones-in-schools-what-they-are-and-how-they-work/

Although phones connect us, ongoing notifications and screen usage can increase stress, overstimulation, and mental exhaustion, hindering our ability to remain present. Creating phone-free areas in your home can effectively provide a mental break, allowing you to distance yourself from distractions and regain concentration. With time, these disconnected areas can enhance tranquility, focus, and general wellness.

Start With the Living Room and Bedroom

To enjoy the advantages of phone-free areas, Melissa Legere, clinical director and co-founder of California Behavioral Health, suggests beginning by defining the purpose of the space for you, whether it's for relaxation, connection, or finding inspiration. From that point, utilize environmental nudging—gently modifying your environment to influence actions—to reduce the urge to grab an invisible phone and encourage staying engaged. “If you have a designated living room area for connection, for instance, eliminate digital distractions and replace them with intentional conversation starters such as board games, photo albums, or an assortment of intriguing magazines to foster dialogue.”

How To Make Phone-Free Zones Work

"Two of the most significant places to designate as screen-free are the dinner table and bedrooms, as these areas significantly affect communication and sleep quality," states Jake Bergstedt, a therapist at Uff Da Mental Health. Therefore, designate those locations as phone-free zones initially.

To organize time in those phone-free areas, begin with small steps but remain consistent. Legere recommends setting aside a fixed 30-minute period each day, such as just before sleeping or during mealtime, and committing it to an offline activity. This could involve reading a tangible book, playing a vinyl record, doing some stretches, or reconnecting with a relative. "This will condition your mind to expect and even desire the breaks," she states. “The goal isn't just to avoid using devices, but to intentionally fill that time with something rejuvenating and turn unplugging into a beneficial experience.”

5 Mental Health Benefits of Creating Phone-Free Zones

  1. Reduced Stress and Cognitive Overload
    • Each ping or vibration from your phone evokes a stress reaction, even if you're unaware of it, and the continuous flow of notifications keeps your mind vigilant. Devices prompt you to frequently alternate between texts, social platforms, and work notifications, causing this constant switching to deplete mental energy. “Whenever the brain remains in active mode continuously, it raises the stress hormone cortisol,” states Kiana Shelton, a therapist and licensed clinical social worker at Mindpath Health. “Phone-free areas provide your mind with moments of reduced stimulation, which may gradually help in lowering stress levels.” By eliminating the mental juggling, serenity and clarity can be regained.
  2. Improve Sleep Quality
    • Areas without phones enhance sleep quality by decreasing the physical, mental, and emotional stimuli that keep your mind active when it needs to relax. "Legere states that since phone screens release blue light that inhibits melatonin production, removing your phone from your bedroom can assist you in managing your natural sleep-wake rhythm more effectively." "This division also enables your mind to unwind by reducing the interruptions from alerts and scrolling that may induce anxiety and disrupt your sleep." A sanctuary without technology indicates to your brain that the bedroom is meant for relaxation, resulting in deeper, more restorative rest.
  3. Stronger Relationships and Deeper Connections
    • “By putting our phones down, we reduce considerable distances, both mentally and physically, from the person we are with,” Legere notes, emphasizing that setting devices aside facilitates engagement by allowing for eye contact, recognizing facial expressions, and tracking conversational cues without the distraction of buzzing notifications or incoming messages. These brief instances of focus are vital for sensing being appreciated and valued. According to Legere, that type of presence indicates to our valued individuals that their presence is important, enhancing the emotional connections that deepen relationships.
  4. Enhanced Creativity and Imaginative Play
    • In the absence of the continual noise of notifications, browsing, or instant gratification, areas free of phones allow your mind to explore, and this mental space can significantly boost creativity for both adults and children. Legere states, “This condition referred to as 'productive boredom' can foster creativity and lead to the emergence of new activities, hobbies, projects, or types of active and imaginative play.” While you take time to doodle, journal, create music, or participate in other tactile activities, those sensory and physical experiences stimulate various brain regions, frequently igniting new thoughts.
  5. Greater Mindfulness and Presence
    • “Taking away devices helps us to be more attuned to our environment and the current moment,” says Legere. Without a screen vying for your focus, it's simpler to observe the subtle details that typically go unnoticed, such as the aroma of a lit candle or the soft buzz of your environment, all of which can enhance your feeling of being grounded and present. "Legere adds that this practice of being present enhances our awareness not just in our daily experiences, but also in the current moment."

Making Phone-Free Zones Stick

Experts agree that maintaining consistency is essential after establishing clear expectations to ensure the habit of using phone-free zones becomes lasting. Bergstedt states, “It’s common for children and adults to feel uneasy initially; change interrupts established routines.” “However, adhering to the limits is what ensures the new routine becomes established.” When establishing these zones as a family, demonstrating the behavior is equally crucial, as children adopt their technology habits from adult actions, not merely from verbal instructions. If you find yourself habitually checking your phone, grabbing it during breaks, or ignoring offline pursuits, Bergstedt advises not to feel disheartened. “Similar to any habit, this requires consistency, patience, and kindness toward oneself.
