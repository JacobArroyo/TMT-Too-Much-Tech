Social Comparison

Author: Joeshua Louis S. Villarta

Source: https://www.psychologs.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/website-12.jpg

In the digital age, we often find ourselves measuring our own lives against the "perfect" photos we see on our screens. This is known as social comparison, and while it is a natural human habit to look at others to see how we are doing, social media has "supercharged" this feeling. Because we have constant access to thousands of people through our phones, we are no longer just comparing ourselves to our neighbors, but to filtered versions of people all over the world.

There are several factors that make social media a breeding ground for these feelings:

  • Curated Profiles: Users selectively share only their best moments, achievements, and edited photos, creating a "highlight reel" that isn't real life.
  • Feedback Metrics: Features like "likes," "comments," and "follower counts" act as public scores that people use to judge their own worth.
  • Algorithmic Curation: Computer programs prioritize showing content that is exciting or enviable to keep you engaged, which often highlights unrealistic lifestyles.
  • Identity Formation: Especially for young people, the need for belonging and acceptance makes them rely on online feedback to build their self-image.
  • Digital Intimacy: Influencers share personal details to feel like "friends" to their followers, making their unattainable lives seem like a standard we should reach.

The disadvantages of falling into this comparison trap can be very harmful to our mental health:

  • Increased Risk of Depression: Feeling envy toward what others post is closely linked to feeling down and worsening mental health.
  • Lower Self-Esteem: Constantly seeing people who seem "better" or more successful can lead to deep feelings of inferiority.
  • Poor Body Image: Looking at idealized and edited body types can lead to a desire for unrealistic physical changes or disordered eating.
  • Anxiety and Stress: The pressure to keep up with others and monitor your own "rank" online keeps the mind in a state of high alert.
  • Academic Decline: For students, the distraction and emotional toll of comparing themselves to others can hurt their ability to focus on school.
  • Financial and Physical Strain: Some may engage in risky habits, like overspending or extreme dieting, just to try and match the lifestyle they see on their feeds.

Social media platforms are specifically engineered to capture and retain our attention by exploiting these psychological tendencies. The "like" button and personalized feeds are deliberate choices made by developers to optimize user engagement, often at the cost of our emotional well-being. This creates a feedback loop where the more we respond to content that makes us compare ourselves, the more the algorithm shows us that exact type of content.

Beyond the computer programs, social media influencers play a huge role in how we feel about ourselves. These creators often present a perfect version of life that is very different from what most people experience every day. Because they act like they are our "friends" by sharing their thoughts and feelings, we tend to compare ourselves to them more easily than we would to a movie star. This can make their luxury travels and flawless looks feel like a normal standard that we are failing to meet, even though it is just a carefully made persona.

To protect your well-being, it is essential to remember that what you see online is often a carefully crafted performance rather than an authentic representation. By setting limits on how much time you spend scrolling and choosing to follow accounts that inspire you rather than make you feel bad, you can break the cycle of comparison. Focusing on your real-life relationships and being grateful for what you have can help you realize that your worth is not measured by a like button.
