Dark Patterns

Author: Joeshua Louis S. Villarta

Source: https://gironajs.com/en/blog/what-are-dark-patterns

Have you ever felt like your phone was playing a trick on you to keep you from closing an app? This is not just your imagination; it is a design strategy called dark patterns. These are sneaky tricks used in the layout of websites and social media apps that are meant to confuse you or push you into doing things you didn't plan to do, like spending more time online or sharing more personal data. By understanding these "hidden traps," you can stop being a target of digital manipulation and start taking back control of your screen time.

These sneaky designs are not accidents; they are built using specific factors to make sure you stay engaged for as long as possible:

  • Visual Interference: Apps often hide the "X" button or make it very small while making the "Stay" or "Accept" buttons big and bright.
  • Roach Motel Design: This makes it very easy to get into a situation, like signing up for a service, but nearly impossible to get out or delete your account.
  • Forced Action: Some platforms make you perform a specific task, like giving up your contact list, before you are allowed to use a certain feature.
  • Social Proof Pressure: Features like "seen" receipts or "streaks" make you feel like you have to respond immediately to avoid letting others down.
  • Confirmshaming: This is when an app uses guilt to make you do something, such as a button that says, "No thanks, I prefer to stay uninformed," when you try to decline a newsletter.
  • Sneak into Basket: This happens when an app adds extra items or permissions to your settings without clearly telling you.

While these tricks might seem small, they lead to many disadvantages for your digital life and mental health:

  • Loss of Privacy: Dark patterns often trick you into sharing much more personal information than you intended to.
  • Digital Fatigue: Constant tricks to keep you scrolling lead to a tired mind and a feeling that you have no control over your time.
  • Financial Loss: Sneaky designs can lead to "hidden costs" where you accidentally sign up for subscriptions you didn't want.
  • Reduced Trust: When you realize a platform is tricking you, it ruins the relationship between the user and the technology.
  • Emotional Stress: Being "shamed" or pressured by an app can lead to feelings of anxiety and guilt.
  • Wasted Time: You may end up spending hours on an app because the "exit" was intentionally made hard to find.

The main goal of these patterns is to turn your attention into profit for the company, often at the cost of your well-being. Some platforms will ask you confusing questions just to get you to click "Yes" on something that benefits them, not you. Because these tricks are so common, many countries are now starting to pass laws to stop these unfair designs, but the best protection is still your own awareness.

Beyond just keeping you on the app, dark patterns work together in combinations that make them even more powerful. For example, an app might use "forced action" to make you create an account, then use "visual interference" to hide the privacy settings, and finally use "confirmshaming" to stop you from leaving. These layers of tricks make it feel like the app is making decisions for you, which can lead to a "dark side" of social media where your choices are no longer your own.

By recognizing these tactics, you can prioritize your digital well-being and make better choices about what information you share online. Instead of just clicking the first button you see, take a second to look for the hidden "No" or the tiny "X" in the corner. Breaking free from these traps means you get to decide when to put your phone down and what parts of your life stay private.
