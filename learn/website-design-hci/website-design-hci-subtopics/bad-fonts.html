Bad Fonts

Author: Heaven Lee Chryzler R. Francisco

Source: https://thepipsqueaker.wordpress.com/2013/02/11/does-your-choice-of-font-signify-you-as-a-terrifying-beast/

Fonts play a crucial role in Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) because they directly affect how users read, understand, and interact with digital content. While typography is often seen as a visual design choice, it is also a key factor in usability and accessibility. Poor font choices can make interfaces difficult to read, reduce comprehension, and negatively impact the overall user experience.

In digital interfaces, users rely heavily on text to navigate, understand instructions, and complete tasks. If the font is hard to read, too decorative, or poorly spaced, users may struggle to process information efficiently. This can lead to frustration, increased errors, and even cause users to leave a website or application entirely.

Bad fonts are not just about choosing an “ugly” typeface. The problem often lies in how the font is used. For example, overly stylized fonts may look appealing in design but can reduce readability, especially for long paragraphs. Similarly, fonts that are too small or have insufficient spacing between letters and lines can strain the eyes and make reading uncomfortable.

Another issue arises when designers use inconsistent typography across an interface. Switching between too many font styles or sizes can confuse users and disrupt the visual hierarchy. Instead of guiding the user’s attention, inconsistent fonts make it harder to distinguish important information from less important content.

Fonts also play a significant role in accessibility. Users with visual impairments, dyslexia, or other reading difficulties may find certain fonts particularly challenging. For instance, fonts with similar-looking characters (such as “I”, “l”, and “1”) can cause confusion. Low contrast between text and background further worsens readability, especially for users with poor vision.

In some cases, designers prioritize aesthetics over usability, choosing fonts that match branding but fail to meet readability standards. While branding is important, it should not come at the cost of user experience. A well-designed interface balances visual appeal with clarity and function.

There are several common characteristics that make fonts problematic in HCI:

  • Overly decorative or script fonts that are hard to read
  • Very small font sizes that strain the eyes
  • Poor spacing between letters or lines
  • Low contrast between text and background
  • Inconsistent font styles across the interface

To avoid these issues, designers should prioritize clarity and readability. Simple, clean fonts are generally more effective for digital interfaces. Proper spacing, sufficient contrast, and consistent typography help users read comfortably and navigate content with ease. Additionally, considering accessibility guidelines ensures that interfaces are usable for a wider range of users.

In conclusion, font choice is a critical aspect of HCI that directly impacts usability and user satisfaction. Bad fonts can reduce readability, increase cognitive effort, and create barriers for users. By focusing on clear, consistent, and accessible typography, designers can create interfaces that are both visually appealing and easy to use.
