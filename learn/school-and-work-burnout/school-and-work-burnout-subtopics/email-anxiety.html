Email Anxiety

Author: Miko Antonio S. Racho

Digital messaging apps like Slack, Teams, and email have become a source of "chronic micro-stress." Unlike a phone call that has a clear beginning and an end, a digital inbox is an infinite, ever-growing list of "demands" that stays with you everywhere, making it hard to find mental peace.

This constant stream of messages creates a state of perpetual alertness, where the brain is always scanning for new notifications or pending tasks. Even when not actively checking, individuals may feel a lingering sense of pressure or urgency, which contributes to anxiety and mental fatigue. Over time, this ongoing micro-stress can impair focus, reduce productivity, and make it difficult to fully engage in deep work or personal downtime.

Another challenge is the blurring of boundaries between work and personal life. Because digital messaging apps are accessible on smartphones, tablets, and computers, the expectation of immediate responses can follow people home or into leisure time. This “always-available” mentality reinforces the stress cycle, leaving little room for mental recovery and increasing the risk of burnout. Individuals may find themselves responding reflexively to messages rather than intentionally prioritizing tasks.

  • The Zeigarnik Effect: The human brain is hardwired to remember "uncompleted tasks" better than completed ones. Every unread notification is an "open digital loop." If you have dozens of unread messages, your brain is trying to keep all those loops open, creating a constant hum of background anxiety.
  • Imagined Urgency: Digital communication creates an "expectation of immediacy." Research shows that most people feel they need to respond to a message within an hour, even if the sender doesn't expect it. This creates a state of constant interruption that prevents you from finishing your actual work.
  • Loss of Agency: When you start your day by checking your inbox, you are letting other people's digital priorities dictate your schedule. This leads to a feeling of losing control over your own time and energy before the day has even truly begun.
  • Attention Hijacking: Notifications and alerts are designed to capture your focus instantly, pulling you away from intentional tasks and reinforcing reactive behavior.
  • Completion Anxiety: The constant pressure to “clear” notifications creates stress, making it feel like tasks are never truly finished even after productive work.

To reduce this anxiety, try "Batching" only checking your digital messages at specific times (like 10:00 AM and 4:00 PM) instead of leaving them open all day.
