Multitasking Myth

Author: Miko Antonio S. Racho

Multitasking Myth

Source: https://infospike.co.uk/the-myth-of-multitasking-why-doing-more-means-getting-less-done/

The belief that humans can "multitask" is a damaging lie in modern digital productivity culture. Many students and professionals pride themselves on having dozens of tabs open coding in one, checking documentation in another, and messaging in a third but neuroscience confirms that the human brain lacks the architecture for parallel processing of high-level cognitive tasks. Instead, the brain engages in "context switching," which is a rapid toggling between different tasks that drains your mental battery.

Context switching comes with hidden costs that go beyond mere time loss. Each time the brain shifts focus from one task to another, it requires a brief but significant period to reorient, recall relevant information, and re-establish mental flow. Over the course of a day, these tiny interruptions accumulate, reducing overall efficiency and increasing cognitive fatigue. What feels like multitasking is often just rapid task hopping, leaving individuals more prone to errors, forgetfulness, and diminished creativity.

Moreover, constant context switching impacts long-term focus and learning. Engaging deeply with complex tasks like problem-solving, writing, or strategic thinking requires sustained attention to build connections and insights. When attention is fragmented across multiple digital windows or notifications, the brain cannot fully engage with any one task, leading to shallow understanding and surface-level results. This not only undermines productivity but can also contribute to frustration and burnout over time.

  • The Switching Cost: Every time you switch from a coding project to a notification, your brain must "re-load" the rules and logic for that task. Research confirms that shifting between tasks can consume as much as 40% of a person's productive time because of the time lost in "resetting."
  • Attention Residue: When you transition from Task A to Task B, your attention doesn't follow immediately. A portion of your cognitive power remains stuck on the previous screen or problem. This "residue" prevents you from reaching a state of "Deep Work," where the most significant learning and complex problem-solving happen.
  • Increased Errors: Because the brain is constantly resetting, it is more likely to make small, frustrating mistakes. For a technical student, this could mean a missing semicolon in a script or a logic error in a query. Research indicates multitasking increases error rates by up to 50% compared to focused work.
  • Decision Fatigue: Constantly choosing between tasks, notifications, and priorities drains mental energy, reducing the ability to make effective decisions over time.
  • Shallow Work Dominance: Frequent interruptions push students toward quick, low-effort tasks instead of engaging in deeper, more meaningful work that builds real understanding.

The solution is "Single-Tasking" setting a timer for a specific window to work on one goal and closing all other digital distractions to protect your brain from the exhaustion of constant switching.
