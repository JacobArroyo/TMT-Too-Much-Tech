Workload

Author: Miko Antonio S. Racho

Source: https://www.ppconsulting.co.uk/blog/2016/stressed-and-overworked-managers-struggle-in-always-on-work-culture/

A digital workload refers to the cumulative mental and emotional strain resulting from "excessive screen time" and "constant connectivity." In modern learning and professional environments, the ease of communication and information access has led to a state of "always-on" productivity, where individuals feel compelled to be "perpetually responsive." This constant influx of information can lead to "cognitive overload" and a loss of mental clarity.

This constant influx of information also fragments attention, making it difficult to engage in "deep, focused work." Notifications from emails, social media, and messaging platforms interrupt cognitive flow, forcing the brain to repeatedly switch contexts. Over time, this “task-switching” reduces productivity and increases mental fatigue, as the brain expends extra energy just to refocus on the original task. As a result, even simple assignments can feel more demanding and time-consuming than they actually are.

Moreover, "information overload" can negatively impact decision-making and critical thinking. When individuals are presented with too much data, they may struggle to distinguish between what is relevant and what is not. This often leads to "analysis paralysis," where the fear of making the wrong choice prevents any decision at all. In academic and professional settings, this can reduce efficiency and confidence, as individuals second-guess their judgments or rely on incomplete understanding.

  1. Cognitive Load Theory: Our working memory is like a small digital buffer. If you pour a gallon of water into it at once, most of it spills. When we try to learn too many things simultaneously scanning articles while watching tutorials we fail to move that information into long-term memory, leading to poor retention.
  2. Analysis Paralysis: Too much information leads to "decision fatigue." When the brain is overwhelmed by digital choices, it shuts down its executive function. This is why simple decisions, like what to eat for dinner or which movie to watch, feel impossible after a long day of processing data.
  3. The Filter Failure: In the digital age, we are losing the ability to distinguish between "signal" (important info) and "noise" (distractions). This leads to a state of mental "clutter" where your brain is busy processing useless facts instead of focusing on your core academic or career goals.
  4. Fragmented Understanding: Consuming information in disconnected chunks (videos, posts, tabs) prevents building a coherent mental framework, leading to shallow comprehension.
  5. Retention Decay: Without focused review and repetition, overloaded information fades quickly from memory, reducing long-term learning effectiveness.

To manage this, practice "Information Curation" follow only a few high-quality sources of information and use "Read-It-Later" apps to save content for a time when you can actually focus on it, instead of trying to process it all at once.
