Zoom Fatigue

Author: Miko Antonio S. Racho

Source: https://www.cnet.com/health/personal-care/excessive-screen-time-costs-billions-report-says-heres-what-it-costs-your-health/

In the modern digital classroom and workplace, the "video call" has become the primary way we connect, but it comes with a hidden psychological cost. "Zoom fatigue" is the mental exhaustion caused by the unique cognitive demands of video conferencing. Unlike in-person communication, where the brain processes social cues almost subconsciously, virtual meetings force the brain into a state of "continuous partial attention" as it struggles to decode human interaction through a digital filter.

One major contributor to "Zoom fatigue" is the constant "self-awareness" that comes from seeing one’s own image on screen. Unlike face-to-face interactions, where people are not typically confronted with their own appearance in real time, video calls create a "mirror-like effect" that can lead to increased "self-consciousness" and stress. Individuals may become overly focused on how they look, their facial expressions, or how they are being perceived by others, which adds an extra layer of cognitive load during conversations.

Another factor is the reduced quality and delay of "nonverbal cues" in virtual settings. Subtle signals such as body language, eye contact, and tone can be harder to interpret due to screen limitations, camera angles, or internet lag. As a result, participants must exert more effort to understand others and respond appropriately, which can be mentally draining over extended periods. This "constant need" to compensate for missing or unclear cues makes even simple interactions feel more demanding than they would in person.

  1. Non-Verbal Overload: In a physical room, you can glance at a speaker or look out a window. On a screen, the "constant gaze" of a dozen digital faces triggers a "hyper-arousal" state in the brain. We are evolutionarily wired to interpret a large face staring directly at us as a potential threat; doing this for hours is physiologically draining.
  2. The Mirror Anxiety: Most platforms show a "self-view" box. Psychologically, this is equivalent to having a mirror held up to your face during every conversation. This leads to "self-evaluation fatigue," where you are constantly monitoring your own expressions and posture rather than focusing on the actual content of the lesson.
  3. Reduced Mobility: Video calls trap you in a narrow digital "cone" of visibility. In-person meetings allow for shifting, pacing, or leaning back, but digital calls require you to stay unnaturally still to remain in frame, which creates physical tension and limits the brain's ability to process information through movement.
  4. Audio Lag Fatigue: Slight delays in sound disrupt natural conversation flow, forcing the brain to work harder to interpret timing and intent, leading to quicker mental exhaustion.
  5. Context Collapse: Digital meetings flatten social cues and environment, making it harder to read the room, interpret emotions, and engage naturally in discussions.

    6. To combat this, experts suggest turning off your "self-view" so you aren't staring at yourself and taking "audio-only" breaks whenever possible to allow your brain to process information more naturally.
