Online Class Stress

Author: Miko Antonio S. Racho

Source: https://internetteguvendekalin.com/facebook-youtube-skype-uzerinden-sosyal-medya-santaji/

Online class stress is the mental and emotional fatigue resulting from the unique demands of "virtual learning environments." Unlike traditional classrooms, online learning often requires students to be "digitally present" for extended periods, leading to a state of "screen fatigue" and reduced focus. The transition from in-person to virtual education has introduced new stressors that can impact academic performance and mental well-being.

Another significant stressor in online learning is the loss of "social connection." In traditional classrooms, students benefit from spontaneous interactions, group discussions, and peer support that help reinforce understanding and motivation. In "virtual settings," these opportunities are limited, making it harder to feel engaged or supported. The lack of immediate feedback from instructors and classmates can leave students uncertain about their progress, increasing anxiety and reducing overall learning satisfaction.

Another stressor is the demand for heightened "self-discipline" and "time management." Without the structure of a physical classroom, students must independently organize schedules, stay on top of assignments, and minimize distractions at home. For many, this sudden responsibility can be overwhelming, especially when combined with the cognitive strain of prolonged screen time. The mental load of juggling technology, deadlines, and self-motivation contributes to fatigue and burnout, which can negatively impact both academic performance and personal well-being.

  • Isolation and Motivation: In a physical classroom, peer energy provides a "social lubricant" that makes work feel easier. Online, you are often alone in your room, which increases the "effort-to-reward" ratio. Without seeing others work, staying motivated requires significantly more willpower.
  • Technostress: This is the constant underlying anxiety caused by the unreliability of hardware, software, or internet connectivity. The fear that your Wi-Fi will drop during a high-stakes digital quiz adds a "secondary layer" of stress that has nothing to do with the actual lesson.
  • The Black Box Effect: Staring at a screen full of black boxes (where peers have their cameras off) creates a sense of academic alienation. The lack of immediate, tactile feedback from instructors makes students feel like they are shouting into a digital vacuum.
  • Accountability Drift: Without physical presence and real-time supervision, it’s easier to lose discipline, delay tasks, and disengage from coursework.
  • Feedback Delay: Limited or slower responses from instructors reduce clarity and momentum, making it harder to stay confident and on track.

    • Creating a "study-only" zone in your house and scheduling regular face-to-face meetups with classmates can help reduce the feelings of isolation associated with virtual learning.
