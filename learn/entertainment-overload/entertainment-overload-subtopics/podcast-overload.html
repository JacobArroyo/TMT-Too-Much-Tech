Podcast Overload

Author: Darene Hanz H. Tunod

Source: https://www.open.edu.au/advice/insights/how-can-you-start-your-own-podcast

Not all research says podcasts are a bad thing, and for a lot of people, they are actually a lifesaver . Listening to a favorite host often feels like hanging out with a friend, which is great if you are feeling a bit lonely or just need some company while doing boring stuff. If you actually focus on a story or a topic you love, it can make you feel way more satisfied and connected to the world . When you listen because you truly want to hear what is being said, it works as a great way to learn or escape for a bit .

When Listening Stops Being Restful:

  • Active Engagement: Deeply focusing on one story helps the brain stay sharp and actually lowers daily boredom.
  • The Noise Trap: Using talk as background noise 24/7 prevents the brain from reaching a state of true rest.
  • Content Debt: Having a massive library of unplayed shows creates a weird sense of pressure and obligation.

The real issue is the "noise trap" that we fall into every day. Most of us just use podcasts as background noise while we do other things, but they often end up being just another thing to deal with. For people in high-stress jobs, studies show that podcasts do almost nothing to stop them from feeling burnt out . Instead of actually resting, they are just filling every quiet moment with more talking, which doesn't let the brain recover. It is like you are never truly off the clock because your ears are constantly processing information.

We also fall for the multitasking myth where we think we are being smart by learning while we work. In reality, having constant noise in your ears does not lower your stress levels at all. Piling more info onto an already tired brain just makes it harder to feel better at the end of the day. You cannot just use a wellness show to fix a bad day when your head is already full of noise from your actual work. It ends up being a heavy "information fatigue" where you hit a point where you stop learning and just start feeling wiped out .

Then there is the choice trap which creates a weird kind of content debt. Having a huge pile of unplayed shows in your library starts to feel like a list of chores you haven't finished yet. When you feel pressure to finish a three-hour episode just to stay in the loop with your friends, the fun is totally gone. You are left with a brain that is too tired to take in anything else, turning a simple podcast into just another item on your to-do list.
