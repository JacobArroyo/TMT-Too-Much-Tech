Tiktok Addiction

Author: Darene Hanz H. Tunod

Source: https://gamequitters.com/tiktok-addiction/

TikTok is basically designed for fast consumption since it uses short videos that make you want to keep scrolling forever. A 2025 study in Health Psychology Research calls this Problematic TikTok Use. It is a state where people feel a compulsive need to stay on the app even when they are exhausted. Since everything is so quick and easy to watch, it is easy to lose track of time. What starts as a five-minute break usually turns into hours of wasted energy.

Key Effects of TikTok Overuse:

  • Emotional Attachment: Users develop a strong tie to the app, leading to mental preoccupation even when offline.
  • The Social Spiral: Addictive design can pull users into stressful loops like cyberbullying or body comparison.
  • The Infinite Loop: No clear stopping points make leaving the app harder than continuing.

Research on Problematic TikTok Use (PTU) shows the app is designed to be more than a quick distraction. By creating a constant need for feedback and emotional engagement, it turns leisure into a mental burden that continues even after closing the app. This cycle leads to attention issues many students experience today. When a platform traps you in a social spiral, it stops being relaxing and becomes a source of stress and procrastination.

A major reason for this is the Variable Reward Schedule built into the algorithm. Because you never know what the next video will bring, your brain stays in a high-arousal “hunting” mode that makes it hard to stop. This is the same principle used in slot machines. Each swipe gives a small dopamine hit, and because videos are short, your brain keeps saying "just one more".

This habit also creates Cognitive Switching Penalties. You might jump from a sad news story to a joke to a cooking video in seconds. Your brain burns energy constantly switching contexts. Instead of resting, it is doing a high-intensity mental workout. That is why after scrolling, you feel mentally drained and real tasks feel harder to start.

Managing this habit requires understanding how the app works. Real rest comes from low-arousal activities where your brain can slow down. TikTok does the opposite by overloading you with rapid information. When a platform removes natural stopping points, it stops being entertainment and becomes a source of mental exhaustion that carries into real life.
