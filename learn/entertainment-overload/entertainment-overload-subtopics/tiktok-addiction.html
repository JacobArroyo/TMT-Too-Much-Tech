Tiktok Addiction

Author: Darene Hanz H. Tunod

TikTok is basically designed for fast watching since it uses short videos that make you want to keep scrolling forever. A 2025 study in Health Psychology Research calls this Problematic TikTok Use. It is a state where people feel a weird, compulsive need to stay on the app even when they are exhausted. Since everything is so quick and easy to watch, it is way too easy to lose track of time. What starts as a five-minute break usually ends up being hours of wasted energy.

  • Emotional Attachment: Users develop a deep tie to the app, leading to "mental preoccupation" where they think about videos even when the phone is off.
  • The Social Spiral: The addictive design often leads to a loss of self-control, pulling users into stressful loops of cyberbullying or body shaming.
  • The Infinite Loop: The lack of "stopping points" in the interface makes the effort to leave the app feel harder than the effort to keep scrolling.

The research on PTU proves that the app is designed to be more than just a quick distraction. By creating a constant need for feedback and emotional engagement, TikTok turns leisure into a mental burden that users carry even after they close the app. This cycle of rumination and instant gratification is what eventually leads to the "attention deficit" many students experience today. When a platform is built to keep you in a social spiral, it stops being a way to unwind and becomes a source of procrastination and stress.

A major reason for this "mental burden" is the Variable Reward Schedule built into the algorithm. Because you never know if the next video will be hilarious, boring, or life-changing, your brain stays in a high-arousal "hunting" mode that makes it physically difficult to put the phone down. This is the same psychological trick used by slot machines to keep people gambling. Each swipe provides a tiny hit of dopamine, and because the videos are so short, your brain justifies "just one more" because the time investment feels low.

This habit also creates what researchers call Cognitive Switching Penalties. When you scroll, you might jump from a sad news story to a comedy skit to a cooking hack in under sixty seconds. Your brain uses up its daily energy reserves just trying to process the context of fifty different topics in ten minutes. Instead of resting your mind, you are actually giving it a high-intensity workout. By the time you finally stop scrolling, you feel "fried" rather than refreshed, which is why your actual work or school projects feel so much more difficult to start.

Ultimately, managing this digital habit requires more than just willpower; it requires recognizing how the app’s own structure works against our ability to rest. Real rest comes from "low-arousal" activities where the brain can process information slowly. TikTok does the exact opposite by firing information at you faster than you can think. When a platform is designed to remove every possible "exit point," it stops being a tool for entertainment and starts being a source of real mental exhaustion that carries over into your real-world responsibilities.
