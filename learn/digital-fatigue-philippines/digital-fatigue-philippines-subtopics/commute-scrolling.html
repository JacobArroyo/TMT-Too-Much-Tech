Commute Scrolling

Author: Ian Gabriel V. Marquez

Source: https://www.philstar.com/business/2023/04/10/2257834/report-58-m-filipinos-spent-44-million-hours-using-e-wallets-2022

The use of smartphones while commuting from work has become embedded in the everyday life of commuters. Evidence has shown that, in general, mobile use provides various benefits to people, such as social connection, acquiring new information,entertainment, and relaxation, while disadvantages relate to the constant availability and connectivity it allows. Unfortunately, smartphone use represents risky behavior in the form of distractions, such as looking at the phone, conversing on the phone, or listening to music, while commuting whether it involves driving cars, bicycles, motorcycles, or walking. Active use of smartphones causes diminished attention and deficits in road users performance, and smartphone use is one of the main risk factors for accidents among all types of road users, warranting continued research on the psychological causes that play a role.

Commuting in Metro Manila, Philippines, is always a challenge. In 2013, a public transportation navigation application, Sakay.ph, which provides commuters with different routes to their destinations was launched. To ensure a user-centered design application and identify issues in the current design, a usability analysis was conducted.

The commuting experience after work has been previously linked to higher experienced stress among employees, not only due to the time and energy spent traveling but also due to the increased work home spillover that occurs during commuting. Simultaneously, the use of smartphones blurs the boundaries between work and home, challenging the ability to achieve a healthy work-life balance through after-work recovery experiences. Employees who routinely stay connected to their work through their smartphones struggle to detach psychologically from work and experience higher levels of exhaustion. This is largely due to mobile use causing the continuous activation of work-related thoughts and preventing the ability to switch off from work. Predominantly, research has focused on the effects of smartphone use during and after working hours on the employee's ability to recover. Studies have found that increased smartphone use after work is strongly associated with inefficient recovery, as it leads to significant work-home interference. Taken together, there is limited evidence on the quality of the association between recovery and smartphone use while commuting from work to home. Existing studies mainly focus on how smartphone use relates to work recovery experiences. Acknowledging this, it is hypothesized that daily recovery experiences will be related to increased smartphone use while commuting after work.

The widespread adoption of short-form video platforms, including TikTok, Instagram Reels, Snapchat, and YouTube Shorts, has significantly transformed the ways in which individuals consume digital content. These platforms are characterized by rapidly delivered, algorithmically curated streams of brief videos that promote continuous scrolling and sustained user engagement. Previous research has demonstrated that Web 2.0 tools can enhance skill development and improve learning outcomes. Building on this foundation, it is important to examine whether short-form video platforms influence core cognitive functions such as attention, memory, and mental fatigue. Although these formats are widely appreciated for their accessibility and entertainment value, growing concerns have emerged regarding their potential effects on cognitive functioning, particularly in relation to attention span, memory retention, and levels of mental fatigue.

It may seem insignificant to scroll endlessly through social media feeds and compulsive time waters created in Silicon Valley. Since the term "internet addiction" was originally used, researchers have documented prevalence rates ranging from 0.8% to 26.7% and have suggested connections with a number of variables, including sociodemographics.

Disadvantages

  • Increases risk of accidents due to distraction while walking or driving.
  • Prevents mental rest, leading to higher fatigue after work.
  • Blurs the boundary between work and personal life.
  • Reduces awareness of surroundings, risking personal safety.
  • Contributes to shorter attention span and reduced focus.
  • Leads to overdependence on smartphones and digital content.
